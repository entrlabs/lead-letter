## The Field Note

> [!example] Ask Before You Change
> Before changing a product, process, training program, or policy, ask who feels the constraint first and what they would warn you about next.

A product team can offer more choices and still miss the customer who cannot find a good fit. A manager can ask workers for input and still waste it if no one owns the next step. A county can fund small-business help and still leave owners wandering through disconnected programs.

Today's sources point to the same practical rule: listen where the constraint is first felt.

Harvard Business School Working Knowledge reports on research about cosmetics product lines. The finding is useful beyond makeup: more options do not always mean more people are served. A smaller, better chosen set may fit more customers.

Harvard Business Review shows the workplace version. Frontline employees often see customer friction, broken tools, and slow handoffs early. Their insight only matters when leaders create a route from observation to action.

Brookings shows the founder and community version. Small-business owners need support systems that are coordinated, not a maze of disconnected programs.

GAO shows the public-institution version. Training should be tied to mission needs and evaluated as part of a learning cycle, not treated as a course catalog.

The World Bank gives the global frame. Jobs grow when infrastructure, rules, skills, finance, and firms line up where people live.

That does not mean every complaint is right. It means the first person who feels the friction often sees the second consequence early. The customer knows which option does not fit. The frontline worker sees the tool that adds work. The small owner knows which support system is too hard to navigate. The public employee knows whether training helps the mission or only fills a calendar.

The next action is simple. Before a decision becomes a rollout, ask three questions:

1. Who will feel this constraint first?
2. What do they already know that we have not asked?
3. What second consequence would they warn us about?

The evidence does not prove one universal fix. It supports a better habit: make the people nearest to the work part of the decision before the side effects arrive.

## Mental Model Lens

> [!tip] Thinking Tool: Second-Order Thinking
> **Mental model:** [Second-Order Thinking](https://fs.blog/second-order-thinking/)<br>
> **Useful idea:** Good decisions ask what happens next, not only what happens first.<br>
> **Short quote:** "And then what?"<br>
> **Use today:** Add one second-step question to a decision meeting before you approve the change.

> [!note] Why It Matters
> First-order thinking asks whether a change solves the visible problem. Second-order thinking asks what new problem the change might create.

## Use It Today

> [!question] For Students
> Before you recommend a solution in class or at work, name the person who feels the problem first. Ask what they would notice that your model might miss.

> [!question] For Managers And Founders
> Pick one recurring complaint from customers or frontline workers. Assign one owner to test whether it points to a real constraint.

> [!question] For Educators And Advisors
> Connect one assignment, workshop, or advising move to the real constraint learners must handle next. Then ask how you will know it helped.
