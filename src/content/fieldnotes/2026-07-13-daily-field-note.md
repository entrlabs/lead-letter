> [!summary] The Reader Promise
> Most problems do not start as emergencies. They start as quiet dependencies that everyone treats like background plumbing. This note helps leaders name those dependencies early, update their judgment as facts change, and give people rules they can understand before pressure rises.

## The Field Note

> [!example] Service Starts Before the Rescue
> Good service includes prevention. Leaders protect people by naming dependencies early: cloud systems, skills, course rules, platform habits, and deal pressure.

The daily work of leadership is not only fixing what breaks. It is noticing what people already depend on.

A vendor can become part of the service promise. A course rule can become a student-risk issue. A training plan can become the difference between real skill growth and extra homework. A platform choice can become a duty-of-care question.

The mistake is waiting until the dependency becomes visible through harm. By then, the leader is reacting to the outage, the lawsuit, the confused worker, or the loss of trust.

The better move is plain: name the dependency while it is still quiet. Ask who relies on it, what could change, what support people need, and what rule would make the system easier to understand.

## Mental Model Lens

> [!tip] Thinking Tool: OODA Loop
> **Mental model:** [The OODA Loop: How Fighter Pilots Make Fast and Accurate Decisions](https://fs.blog/ooda-loop/)
> **Useful idea:** Observe, orient, decide, and act is a practical loop for dependency-heavy work because leaders must keep updating what they know before risk hardens into failure.
> **Short quote:** No short quote used.
> **Use today:** Ask what new information should change the next decision about vendors, training, platform design, or classroom rules.

> [!note] Why It Matters
> The OODA Loop is a simple way to keep learning while conditions change: observe what is happening, orient around what it means, decide what to do, and act. For leaders, the useful part is the loop. A dependency is not something to review once and forget. It needs regular attention, because new facts can change the right decision.

## Use It Today

> [!question] For Students
> Pick one system your group depends on. What new information would change the next decision you make about it?

> [!question] For Managers And Founders
> Name one vendor, skill, rule, or platform habit that already shapes trust. What simple check would reveal risk before people feel it?

> [!question] For Educators And Advisors
> Ask learners to compare a hidden dependency with a named one. Which version gives people clearer rules and better support?
